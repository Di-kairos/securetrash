Almost every "secure delete" tool promises to overwrite a file until it's unrecoverable. On a modern SSD that's a lie — wear leveling, copy-on-write and TRIM keep your bytes alive in cells no overwrite can reach. securetrash refuses to pretend.

The myth vs reality

What "secure delete" actually does on an SSD

The overwrite model was true for spinning disks, where a logical block maps to a fixed physical spot. SSDs break that assumption completely.

-Overwrite the file 3× / 7× / 35× and the data is gone forever.
-The drive writes zeros exactly where the original bytes lived.
-TRIM guarantees the old data is erased right away.
+Wear leveling sends your overwrite to a new cell; the original sits in flash, still recoverable.
+Over-provisioned and remapped cells hold copies your OS can't even address.
+Real protection is crypto-shred: destroy the key, and every copy becomes useless noise at once.

Want the full technical breakdown (fact-checked against NIST SP 800-88r2 & the UCSD FAST'11 study)? Read “Why overwriting files doesn't work on SSDs”.

How crypto-shred works

Encrypt first, then throw away the key

If your data only ever exists as ciphertext, the scattered physical copies are noise. Destroy the key and they all die — no need to reach the hidden cells.

01

Create a vault

An encrypted AES-256 sparsebundle. Set it up before you have secrets — it's preventive.

02

Work inside it

Open the vault, use your files. They only ever touch disk as ciphertext.

03

Destroy the key

vault destroy removes the container and the key it depends on.

04

Data is gone

Ciphertext without a key is unrecoverable — regardless of what the flash controller did.
